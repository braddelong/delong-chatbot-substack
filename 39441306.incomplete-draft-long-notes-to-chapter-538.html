I confess I was astonished for Eizenstat when I read this from him about how the Carter White House worked: “The White House needs an economic policy coordinator…. I urged Charles Schultze to move from the CEA… office… to the West Wing of the White House, to serve in the additional role of economic policy coordinator. He refused and, in retrospect, rightly so… it would compromise his objectivity and burden him with administrative duties…. But this left the President with no person without a[n agency] stake in the outcome… charged with coordinating… disparate economic advice…. The interdepartmental Economic Policy Group, chaired by the Treasury… [did] nothing more than… collect… differing agency recommendations, without adequate synthesis. The Ford administration had developed an appropriate model, which we should have employed…”

There was, in the White House, a person without an agency stake in the outcome charged with coordinating disparate domestic-policy advice: Stu Eizenstat. Eizenstat was in the White House, physically. Treasury Secretaries Blumenthal and Miller were not. If Eizenstat was overwhelmed—as he was—he should have called for more assistants, or for a peer.
