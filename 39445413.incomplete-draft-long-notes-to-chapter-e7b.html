Yes, rent-seeking is an important failure mode of big (and small!) government. But how it was that the social-democratic political-economic mode of... distribution? allocation?... could not be sustained for more than three decades in the North Atlantic while the neoliberal mode hangs on—that still surprises me.

And you can concede some valid elements of a neoliberal critique of social democracy without winding up at the right-neoliberal position of Reagan and Thatcher that the relative poverty of the poor is a feature of modern society rather than a bug. In my younger days I was a card-carrying “left-neoliberal”, after all. I believed in:

  • a focus on generating a stable price-level, high-investment, high productivity-growth economy,

  • market means to social-democratic ends where appropriate,

  • an attack on rent-seeking of all kinds, and

  • a reliance on means-tested benefits to diminish the effect of wealth inequalities

That seemed the obviously-right thing to do in the late 1980s and 1990s to return us to the days of the trentes glorieuses, and to assemble a durable political coalition to deal with the fact that an awful lot of people welcomed Thatcher and Reagan. It was a world in which a life-long Lenin apologist like Eric Hobsbawm could also write in his Age of Extremes that:

There was considerable scope for applying the neo-liberal cleansing-agent to the encrusted hull of many a good ship 'Mixed Economy' with beneficial results. Even the British Left was eventually to admit that some of the ruthless shocks imposed on the British economy by Mrs Thatcher had probably been necessary. There were good grounds for some of the disillusion with state-managed industries and public administration that became so common in the 1980s…

Now Ed Balls and I can whimper that the left-neoliberal correction to social democracy was within our grasped, and was in fact almost grasped—that what Gary Gerstle calls the New Deal Order and I call social democracy could have been stabilized via a left-neoliberal correction, rather than being followed, as it was, by a near-hegemonic Right-Neoliberal Order. We can whimper that only the fact that five Supreme Court justices were partisan Republicans in the U.S. in 2000 and that Nick Clegg was Nick Clegg kept us from advancing into the Broad Sunlit Uplands in the US and UK.

But that is not very convincing, is it? Especially since the stubborn persistence of right-neoliberalism now shading into ethno-fascism is present in nearly the entire OECD.

My view is that social democracy ought to work. But—even when in the left-neoliberal sheep's clothing that Clinton and Blair donned—it does not. It is not politically sustainable. And, indeed, it was not sustained.

The fact that I have a hard time believing it is not sustainable and understanding why it was not sustained points to a big gap in my understanding. Yingyi Qian is right in pinpointing this as a flaw in the book.

Having the government establish & maintain a property-rights order & enforce contracts is, after all, a form of “interference” to the anarcho-capitalists. Most stop being happy with police & courts.

So why is police & courts acceptable, while police & courts & demand management is not?

the market giveth; the market taketh away; blessed be the name of the market…

In short, I think you are right, but I do not understand why you are right.
