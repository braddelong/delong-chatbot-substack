I have seen many very bad things written about Twitter vs. Musk, but this struck me—and still strikes me—as the very worst:

J.B. Heaton and M. Todd Henderson: Twitter’s Lawsuit Against Elon Musk Looks Like a Loser: ‘The company probably can’t prove damages, and its shareholders weren’t a party to the agreement… [so Musk] is likely to walk away largely unscathed.... This case will be a good lesson on the limits of boilerplate merger agreements and the difference between a corporation and its shareholders....

Specific performance is used fleetingly… only when there is no alternative.... Delaware courts have rarely ordered specific performance in merger agreements…. What happens if the court orders specific performance and Mr. Musk refuses?... It isn’t Mr. Musk that promised to buy Twitter, but two entities under his control. The court could hold them in criminal contempt, but… “corporations have neither bodies to be punished, nor souls to be condemned.” Mr. Musk promised to “cause” these entities to consummate the deal, but a court is unlikely to jail him if he shirks or refuses. Mr. Musk could play a high-stakes game of chicken that ultimately reveals that courts are extremely limited in cases like this if the parties don’t want to play along....

Damages.... Twitter shareholders are surely worse of.... But the shareholders aren’t party to the agreement. Only Twitter Inc. is a party…. Twitter would have to prove harm, such as lost profits, and that’s an uphill battle…. The… $1 billion breakup fee.... Breakup fees are supposed to reflect damages… [not] act as a penalty…. Twitter isn’t obviously worse off by $1 billion—if at all—a court might balk at imposing such a high fee...

Is there a sentence here that is on the same planet as the truth of the matter?
