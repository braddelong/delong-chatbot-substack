Why male supremacy became so firmly established millennia ago, back in the Agrarian Age, is not obvious: Especially since the degree of patriarchy varied widely. Women appear to have had as visible a place in Ancient Egypt as men—although the powerful women are almost invariably linked to powerful men. In southeast Asia, the southern chunk of Africa, and in much of South America much inheritance was matrilineal, and more social power accrued to women (and aunts and uncles) and less, relatively, to fathers. Places in which resources were scarce, and thus property valuable, tended to place much more attention on controlling female fertility and chastity. There appears to me at least to be something to the idea that patriarchy was greatly strengthened in places influenced by the Indo-European migration and cultural transmission that came out of the steppes. And then there are the places where one man-many wives and female seclusion are the ideal.
